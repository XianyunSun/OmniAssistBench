Your task is to assess the quality of a "CANDIDATE" (the answer waiting to be evaluated) by comparing it against a "GOLD ANSWER" (Ground Truth) and a specific set of "KEY POINTS". If there is "<OR>" tag in the GOLD ANSWER or the KEY POINTS, the candidate is considered correct if it semantically contains any one of the sentences connected by this "<OR>" tag.
You will receive the QUESTION, the CANDITATE, the GOLD ANSWER, and the KEY POINTS. The source video is not provided.

Evaluation Process:
First, analyze the Candidate Answer against the following dimensions. Output your analysis for these dimensions using the labels: Yes / No / Partly.
Question Understanding: Is the candidate a grammatically coherent response to the question, which indicates the model understood the question?
Semantic Alignment: Does the candidate semantically match the Gold Answer?
Polarity Consistency: For evaluative questions, does the candidate share the same sentiment polarity (positive/negative) as the Gold Answer? Label this as "Not Applicable" if the question is not evaluative.
Key Point Coverage: Does the candidate mention all the specific Key Points? Label this as "Not Applicable" if there is only 1 Key Point.
Silence Protocol: If the Gold Answer is "KEEP QUIET," does the candidate correctly choose to remain silent or describe inaction? Label this as "Not Applicable" if the Gold Answer is not "KEEP QUIET".
Redundancy: Does the candidate contain excessive redundant information not supported by the Gold Answer?
Conversational Tone: Does the candidate sound like it is directly talking to a human user?

Scoring Rubric:
Based on your analysis, assign a score from 0 to 5 according to the following standards:
5 - The candidate can be interpreted as a valid answer to the question, indicating comprehension of the prompt. It semantically matches the Gold Answer, although it may use synonyms or paraphrasing. It covers all Key Points. It contains no redundant or irrelevant information, though minor, contextually relevant elaborations are acceptable. The candidate receives a score of 5 if the Gold Answer is "KEEP QUIET", and the candidate gives the exact "[KEEP QUIET]" or "KEEP QUIET" output.

However, the semantic match with the Gold Answer may be imperfect (e.g., including incorrect details compared to the Gold Answer). For descriptive questions, the object or perspective falls into the correct category but may lack precision. It may contain redundant information not present in the Gold Answer, provided that information is still relevant to the core subject. The candidate receives a score of 4 if the Gold Answer is "KEEP QUIET", and the candidate describes inaction, although it fails to output exactly "[KEEP QUIET]" or "KEEP QUIET".

The semantic match with the Gold Answer may be imperfect. The candidate may contain redundant information not mentioned in the Gold Answer, as long as it remains relevant to the general topic of the question. The candidate receives a score of 3 if the Gold Answer is “KEEP QUIET” and the candidate claims that there is insufficient information to answer, or that it is not the appropriate time to respond.
2 - The candidate can be interpreted as a valid answer to the question, indicating comprehension of the prompt. But the candidate fails to provide a correct answer. It directly contradicts the Gold Answer (e.g., opposite polarity for evaluative questions or describing the wrong object for descriptive questions). It may contain obvious irrelevant information. The candidate receives a score of 2 if the Gold Answer is “KEEP QUIET” but the candidate provides a grammatically valid response.

The candidate receives a score of 1 if the Gold Answer is “KEEP QUIET” but the candidate provides a response that is loosely related to the question.
0 - Irrelevant: The candidate is entirely unrelated to the question or the Gold Answer, indicating a complete failure to comprehend the input. The candidate receives a score of 0 if the Gold Answer is “KEEP QUIET” and the candidate provides a response that is completely unrelated to the question.
